Usage-based billing budgets

When budgets overlap, specificity wins.

GitHub’s user-level budget precedence is simple, but the combinations feel noisy. Remember it as one sentence: pick the most specific budget that applies to the user, then ignore the less specific ones for that user.

The decision is not a math problem. It is a lookup.

The common mistake is treating overlapping budgets as additive, lowest-wins, or sequential gates. For user-level budgets, GitHub resolves a single winning budget by specificity.

1. Is there an individual budget?

If yes, that is the user’s effective user-level budget. Stop comparing.

→

2. Else, is there a cost center per-user budget?

If yes, that becomes the effective budget for members of that cost center.

→

3. Else, use universal

The universal user-level budget is the safety-net default.

❌ Not additive$10 universal + $20 cost center does not become $30.
❌ Not lowest-winsA $0 universal does not block a user who has a $25 cost center override.
❌ Not highest-winsA $100 universal does not override a $20 individual budget.
✅ Specificity-winsThe closest matching rule to the user is the effective user-level budget.
Shared explanation: “Universal is the default policy, cost center is the department exception, individual is the named-person exception. The named-person exception wins because it is the most specific.”

Common questions, quick answers

Short explanations for the places people commonly trip up — useful for discussions, internal enablement, or self-serve review.

“If universal is $0, does it block everyone?”

It blocks users who only have the universal budget.

If a cost center or individual budget also applies, the more specific budget wins for that user.

“Do user-level budgets stack?”

No. They resolve to one effective budget.

Think “policy override,” not “wallet balance.” The most specific policy becomes the user’s budget.

“Is the highest dollar amount chosen?”

No. Dollar amount does not determine precedence.

A $5 individual budget beats a $100 universal budget because the individual rule is more specific.
